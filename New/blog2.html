The LG vs The CM

The Lieutenant Governor vs. the Chief Minister

Who is Delhi’s Boss ?

There has been a long running political power tussle between the Centre and the Arvind Kejriwal-led Delhi Government, to establish who will rule Delhi. The tussle started as early as February 2015, after AAP won a landslide majority in the Delhi Assembly. In 2015, the Centre issued a notice stating that the services department of the Delhi government would fall under the L-G's purview. This was in addition to three other subjects – land, police and public order – which fall with the Centre. Within months of the AAP taking power in Delhi in 2015, the BJP-led central government took the anti-corruption unit away from the state government. The centre also withdrew a 1998 concession granted by LK Advani, which required the LG to consult the elected government on every decision relating to police, public order and bureaucratic appointments. Around the same time, AAP bypassed the LG and made several top bureaucratic appointments without consulting L-G Najeeb Jung, who hit back reasserting himself by claiming himself to be the sole authority in matters of appointment.

As per the constitution, the Delhi government does not have any control in matters of land, bureaucracy and the police. For everything else, the constitution says the Lt Governor has to be guided by the aid and advice of the elected government - as is the case in all states. However, unlike in other states, the LG has the power to disagree with the state cabinet and can overrule the state government. In such cases, the LG has to ask the centre to take the call.

AAP government claimed that it is this power that the LG has abused to eat into the elected government’s authority and impede decisions taken by them for stalling administrative and policy decisions affecting daily government work and blocking all important decisions taken by the Delhi government for political reasons. On the other hand, the Centre argued that the Delhi government cannot have absolute powers in the national capital, and especially in respect of its unique position, the responsibility rested on the shoulders of the union Government.

The recent Supreme Court judgement put to rest the power tussle between the Centre and the State governments while stating that Lieutenant Governor of Delhi (presently Anil Baijal) does not have independent decision-making powers and is bound to act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers. The verdict virtually rejected the High Court’s judgement that had ruled in favour of the LG (then Najeeb Jung). The SC verdict also observed that the national capital had a special status and was not a full state.

This verdict can be viewed as a major victory for the AAP-led Delhi government where the Supreme Court has reprimanded the LG for behaving as an ‘obstructionist’ and not giving primacy to the peoples’ mandate. It is only when both the constitutional functionaries, the Delhi government and the LG’s office, work harmoniously with mutual respect for each other, that an accountable, responsive, equitable and inclusive mode of governance, one that is in accordance with the people’s mandate, will be established.
