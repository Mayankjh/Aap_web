Deteriorating AQI in Delhi NCR

Deteriorating AQI in Delhi NCR

Prerna Chopra

An AQI refers to Air Quality Index. An Air Quality Index is a number used by government agencies to communicate to the public how polluted the air currently is, or is forecasted to become. As the AQI increases, an increasingly large percentage of the population is likely to experience increasingly severe adverse health effects.

According to the CPCB (Central Pollution Control Board) report, an AQI of 0-50 is “good”, 51-100 is “satisfactory”, 101-200 is “moderate”, and 201-300 is “poor”.

You may be astonished to read that the current AQI of Delhi is 154, which is considered to be “moderate” according to the CPCB report. But we have been successful in reaching such air quality levels only because of the efforts of the Delhi government, and the rules and regulations imposed by them for the same. There was a time when the air quality of Delhi NCR had reached the “severe” mark on the Air Quality Index (AQI)

In November 2017, Delhi NCR experienced a major dip in the air quality levels. The AQI was recorded to have reached a whooping level of 491, which is considered to be “severe-plus”. There were major reasons for this decline in the air quality in Delhi-NCR:

  • National capital shares its border with the states of Haryana and Uttar Pradesh. One of the major reasons of increasing air pollution levels in Delhi was crop burning by the farmers in these states. Farmers burned rice stubbles in Punjab, Haryana and Uttar Pradesh. It was estimated that approximately 35 million tonnes of crops were set afire by these states. The wind carried all the pollutants and dust particles, which got locked in the air.
  • Pollution caused by the traffic menace in Delhi was another reason contributing to the air pollution and smog. The air quality index had reached “severe” levels. Vehicular emission was increasing the hazardous effects of air pollution and smog. The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and the National Environmental Engineering Research Institute (NEERI) had declared vehicular emission as a major contributor to Delhi’s increasing air pollution.
  • As the winter season arrived, dust particles and pollutants in the air became unable to move. Due to stagnant winds, these pollutants got locked in the air and affected weather conditions, resulting in smog.
  • Investing less on public infrastructure was another reason of air pollution. In India, investment in public transport and infrastructure is low, which leads to congested roads, and hence air pollution.
  • Large-scale construction in Delhi-NCR was another culprit that was increasing dust and pollution in the air.
  • Industrial pollution and garbage dumps were also increasing air pollution and building-up smog in the air.
