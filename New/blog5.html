Waste Management

Delhi’s Mountains of Garbage
-Ayush Chauhan

Delhi is one of the biggest and most populated cities in the world. Qutub Minar, built some odd 800 years ago, is one of the popular tourist destinations of Delhi. But another 'tower' though under construction, would soon overtake the title of being the tallest tower in Delhi or even India. While the Qutub Minar is the pride of the city the same can't be said for Ghazipur landfill site.

As of September 2018, the Ghazipur landfill site, 45 meters higher than the prescribed limit of 20 meter, is merely 8 meters shorter than Qutub Minar. Spreading across 70 acres and housing 12 million tons of waste, it is easily one of the biggest landfill sites in the world proof of our failing waste management system. Owing to the large population of Delhi, waste management remains its biggest concern which if not tackled in time, would soon turn into an epidemic.

Delhi generates around 9,500 tons of waste per day that needs to be disposed safely. But poor management and a failed system have led to much bigger problems that shows no signs of improvement.

Delhi has three major landfill sites namely -- Ghazipur, Okhla and Bhalswa; these three sites have exceeded their capacities almost a decade ago, but are still in use. Searches for new landfill sites are always met with objections raised by people living in the area.

However, the problems do not end just here. The landfills regularly catch fire and release toxic gases. The waste also contaminates the groundwater. Respiratory diseases, pneumonia and tuberculosis are common occurrences for the families living in close premises of these landfills. During monsoon, the runoff water adds to the problems by spreading dengue and swine flu.

Looking for solutions requires not just a total revamp of the existing waste management system, but also huge monetary aids on part of the government. Efficient and cost effective measures need to be adopted that promote a sustainable environment.

The enormous amounts of waste produced can be transformed into electricity by using Waste-to-Energy plants. Compost can be prepared out of household bio-degradable waste, and bio-gas plants could be set up which would reduce the burden on traditional energy resources.

The residents of the city are the cause of the problem and the solution too. It’s only by the joint efforts of the concerned authorities and the people, that this crisis can be tackled.
